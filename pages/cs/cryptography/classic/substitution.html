Classic Substitution Cipher

Introduction

A substitution cipher encrypts messages by replacing each symbol of the plaintext with another symbol from the same alphabet, according to a fixed system defined by a secret key. Unlike transposition ciphers, which rearrange symbols, substitution ciphers transform the symbols themselves.

Mathematically, a substitution cipher defines a bijection $$ f_K : A \to A $$ where \(A\) is the alphabet and \(K\) is the key. The ciphertext sequence is: $$ C_i = f_K(P_i), \quad 1 \le i \le n $$ and decryption uses the inverse mapping: $$ P_i = f_K^{-1}(C_i) $$

Characteristics

  • Each plaintext symbol is replaced independently.
  • Letter frequency is preserved in monoalphabetic substitution, making simple ciphers vulnerable to analysis.
  • Key space depends on the size of the alphabet; for a monoalphabetic cipher over 26 letters, there are 26! possible keys.
  • Polyalphabetic substitution ciphers, such as Vigenère, improve security by varying the substitution across positions.

Common Algorithm Types

Monoalphabetic Substitution

Each plaintext letter maps to a fixed ciphertext letter throughout the message.
Example: 

Plaintext:  HELLO
Key mapping: H->Q, E->X, L->Z, O->M
Ciphertext: QXZZM

Caesar Cipher

A simple monoalphabetic substitution with a fixed shift. Each letter is replaced by another a fixed number of positions down the alphabet. Mathematically, this can be written as:

Encryption: $C_i = (P_i + k) \bmod 26$
Decryption: $P_i = (C_i - k) \bmod 26$ 

Plaintext: HELLO
Shift: 3 (ROT3)
Ciphertext: KHOOR

ROT-n Ciphers

ROT-n ciphers are generalizations of the Caesar cipher where each letter is shifted by n positions. ROT13 is the most common example. 

Plaintext: HELLO
ROT13: URYYB

Atbash Cipher

Atbash is a classical monoalphabetic substitution that maps each letter to its reverse in the alphabet (A↔Z, B↔Y, ...). While technically monoalphabetic, it is often included as a foundational cipher for understanding substitutions. 

Plaintext: HELLO
Ciphertext: SVOOL

Polyalphabetic Substitution

Each position may use a different substitution alphabet, often controlled by a repeating key sequence. The Vigenère cipher is the classic example: $$ C_i = (P_i + K_{i \bmod m}) \bmod n $$ where \(n\) is the alphabet size and \(m\) is the key length.

Homophonic Substitution

Each plaintext symbol can map to multiple ciphertext symbols to flatten frequency distribution. Example: the letter 'E' could be encoded as any of {3,7,12,19}, making frequency analysis more difficult.

Polygraphic Substitution

Groups of letters (digraphs, trigraphs) are substituted as a unit, e.g., the Playfair cipher uses digraphs to increase diffusion.

Mathematical Representation

Let \(P = (P_1, P_2, \dots, P_n) \in A^*\) be the plaintext. A substitution cipher with key \(K\) is a bijective function: $$ f_K : A \to A $$ Extended homomorphically to strings: $$ E_K(P_1, \dots, P_n) = (f_K(P_1), \dots, f_K(P_n)) $$ Decryption applies the inverse function: $$ D_K(C_1, \dots, C_n) = (f_K^{-1}(C_1), \dots, f_K^{-1}(C_n)) $$

analysis

  • Frequency Analysis: Monoalphabetic substitution can be broken using letter frequencies in natural languages.
  • Kasiski Examination: Detects key length in polyalphabetic ciphers such as Vigenère.
  • Pattern Matching: Repeated sequences and known words can help identify substitutions.
  • Homophonic Analysis: More complex frequency methods are required to attack ciphers with multiple ciphertext symbols per plaintext letter.

Conclusion

Substitution ciphers illustrate the principle of confusion in Shannon’s terms by hiding the relationship between plaintext and ciphertext symbols. While simple monoalphabetic schemes are weak, polyalphabetic and polygraphic variants remain historically significant and demonstrate foundational ideas in modern cryptography.
