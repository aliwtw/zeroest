Classical Cryptography Fundamentals

Classical Cipher Properties

Classical ciphers are methods of encrypting messages using systematic transformations. They can be analyzed in terms of their ability to obscure relationships between plaintext, ciphertext, and keys.

  • Substitution Ciphers: Replace each symbol with another symbol from the alphabet.
  • Transposition Ciphers: Rearrange the positions of symbols without changing them.
  • Key Dependence: Encryption depends on a secret key that controls the transformation.

Confusion vs. Diffusion (Shannon)

Claude Shannon introduced two essential principles for secure ciphers:

  • Confusion: Make the relationship between ciphertext and key as complex as possible.
  • Diffusion: Spread the influence of each plaintext symbol across many ciphertext symbols.

Redundancy Exploitation

Natural languages have patterns and statistical regularities. Cryptanalysis often exploits this redundancy:

  • Frequency analysis of letters or digrams.
  • Pattern recognition in repeated sequences.
  • Using known grammatical structure to predict ciphertext.

Mathematical Formalization

Alphabet as a Finite Set

Let the alphabet be a finite set of symbols: $$A = \{a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n\}$$

Kleene Star: $A^*$

$A^*$ represents all finite sequences (strings) of elements from $A$, including the empty string $\epsilon$ (epsilon). $$ A^* = \{\epsilon\} \cup A \cup A^2 \cup A^3 \cup \dots $$

Example: For $A = \{0,1\}$, $A^* = \{\epsilon, 0, 1, 00, 01, 10, 11, 000, \dots\}$

Encryption and Decryption Functions

The encryption function maps plaintext strings to ciphertext strings: $$ E_K: A^* \to A^*, \quad P \mapsto C $$ The decryption function reverses this process: $$ D_K: A^* \to A^*, \quad C \mapsto P $$ satisfying: $D_K(E_K(P)) = P$.

Periodicity and Modular Arithmetic

Many classical ciphers, such as the Caesar cipher, use periodic keys and modular arithmetic: $$ C_i = (P_i + k_i) \bmod n $$ where $P_i$ and $C_i$ are positions of letters in the alphabet, $k_i$ is the key, and $n = |A|$.

Permutation Group Representation: Symmetric Group $S_n$

Transposition ciphers can be modeled as elements of the symmetric group $S_n$, the group of all permutations of $n$ elements:

  • Each transposition corresponds to a permutation in $S_n$.
  • Combining multiple transpositions corresponds to composing permutations.
  • Properties of $S_n$ ensure that each permutation has an inverse, making decryption possible.

Symbolic Substitution Mappings

A substitution cipher is defined by a key $K$ that determines a bijection $$ f_K : A \to A $$ which is extended homomorphically to the free monoid $A^*$ of all finite strings. For a plaintext sequence $P = (P_1, P_2, \dots, P_m) \in A^*$, encryption is $$ C_i = f_K(P_i) \quad \text{for} \quad 1 \leq i \leq m $$ where $C \in A^*$ is the ciphertext. Decryption uses the inverse mapping $f_K^{-1}$ determined by the same key $K$, so that $P_i = f_K^{-1}(C_i)$.

Conclusion

Classical cryptography combines structured transformations of finite alphabets with key-dependent functions. Understanding the mathematical formalization, Shannon’s principles, and permutation-based representations is fundamental for analyzing and designing secure ciphers.
