Classic Transposition Cipher

Introduction

A transposition cipher is a method of encryption where the positions of characters in the plaintext are systematically rearranged according to a key, without altering the actual characters themselves. Unlike substitution ciphers, which change the symbols, transposition ciphers rely solely on permutation of symbol positions to achieve secrecy.

Formally, if the plaintext has length n, the encryption process applies a permutation \(\pi \in S_n\) to the indices of the symbols: $$ C_i = P_{\pi(i)} $$ where \(S_n\) is the symmetric group of all permutations of \(n\) elements.

Characteristics

  • Preserves letter frequencies exactly as it's useful for cryptanalysts, dangerous for cipher security.
  • Security depends on hiding the permutation pattern.
  • Often combined with substitution ciphers in polyalphabetic designs for increased diffusion.

Spartan Scytale

One of the oldest known transposition methods: the message is written along the length of a strip wound around a cylinder of fixed diameter. Reading without the correct diameter produces a scrambled text.

Mathematical Representation

Let \(P = (P_1, P_2, \dots, P_n)\) be the plaintext sequence over alphabet \(A\). A transposition cipher is determined by a permutation \(\pi \in S_n\), and the ciphertext is: $$ C = (P_{\pi(1)}, P_{\pi(2)}, \dots, P_{\pi(n)}) $$ Decryption is performed by applying the inverse permutation \(\pi^{-1}\): $$ P = (C_{\pi^{-1}(1)}, C_{\pi^{-1}(2)}, \dots, C_{\pi^{-1}(n)}) $$

Cryptanalysis

  • Frequency Analysis: Ineffective for breaking pure transposition, since symbol distribution is unchanged.
  • Anagramming: Analysts search for possible rearrangements matching natural language patterns.
  • Known-plaintext attack: If a segment of plaintext is guessed, the permutation can often be deduced.
  • Multiple permutations: Using repeated transpositions can exponentially increase complexity for the attacker.

Conclusion

The transposition cipher exemplifies the concept of diffusion in Shannon's model by spreading the influence of each plaintext letter to different ciphertext positions. While insecure on its own against modern cryptanalysis, it remains an important historical cipher and a building block for composite systems combining substitution and transposition.
