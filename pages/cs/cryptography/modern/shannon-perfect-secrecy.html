Shannon's Perfect Secrecy

Definition

Perfect secrecy, introduced by Claude Shannon in 1949, is a formal notion of unconditional security for encryption schemes. An encryption scheme achieves perfect secrecy if the ciphertext reveals no information about the plaintext, regardless of the adversary's computational power.

Formally, let $(\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{K}, \mathcal{C}, \text{Enc}, \text{Dec})$ be an encryption scheme, where:

  • $\mathcal{M}$: message space
  • $\mathcal{K}$: key space
  • $\mathcal{C}$: ciphertext space
  • $\text{Enc}: \mathcal{K} \times \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{C}$: encryption function
  • $\text{Dec}: \mathcal{K} \times \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{M}$: decryption function, satisfying $\text{Dec}(k, \text{Enc}(k,m)) = m$ for all $m \in \mathcal{M}$ and $k \in \mathcal{K}$

Formal Definition of Perfect Secrecy

An encryption scheme provides perfect secrecy if for every probability distribution over messages $M$ and every ciphertext $c \in \mathcal{C}$:

$$ \Pr[M = m \mid C = c] = \Pr[M = m] \quad \forall m \in \mathcal{M}, \; c \in \mathcal{C}. $$

Equivalently, observing the ciphertext does not change the adversary's knowledge about the plaintext:

$$ M \perp C \quad \text{(message $M$ is independent of ciphertext $C$).} $$

Here, probabilities are taken over the uniform choice of key $K \in \mathcal{K}$ used in encryption:

$$ C = \text{Enc}(K, M), \quad K \sim \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{K}). $$

Properties of Perfect Secrecy

  • Unconditional Security: Security holds against any adversary, regardless of computational power.
  • Key-Length Requirement: To achieve perfect secrecy, the key space must be at least as large as the message space: $$ |\mathcal{K}| \ge |\mathcal{M}|. $$
  • Uniform Key Distribution: Keys must be chosen uniformly at random.
  • One-Time Use: Each key must only be used once; reusing keys compromises secrecy.

Example: One-Time Pad

The one-time pad (OTP) is the canonical example of perfect secrecy. Let messages and keys be $n$-bit strings:

$$ m = (m_1, m_2, \dots, m_n) \in \{0,1\}^n, \quad k = (k_1, k_2, \dots, k_n) \in \{0,1\}^n $$

Encryption is defined as bitwise XOR:

$$ c = \text{Enc}(k, m) = m \oplus k $$

Decryption is also XOR with the same key:

$$ m = \text{Dec}(k, c) = c \oplus k $$

Since $k$ is uniformly random and used only once, each ciphertext $c$ is equally likely for every plaintext $m$, satisfying Shannon’s perfect secrecy condition.

Limitations

  • Requires a key as long as the message.
  • Keys must be securely distributed and never reused.
  • Practical deployment is limited to small-scale applications due to key management constraints.

Conclusion

Shannon's perfect secrecy provides the gold standard for encryption: no information about the plaintext can be inferred from the ciphertext. While unconditionally secure, it imposes strong constraints on key length and management, which is why most practical cryptosystems rely on computational assumptions instead.
